# Product Principles

## 1. Commitment before tracking

Tracking is useful only after the user makes a clear commitment.

Every core flow should answer:

- What will the user do?
- When or how often?
- Who is involved?
- What counts as completion?

## 2. Social accountability must be actionable

Social features should help users act.

Useful interactions include:

- Confirming a commitment
- Encouraging a check-in
- Acknowledging completion
- Helping recover from a miss
- Joining a shared challenge
- Reviewing progress

Vanity engagement should not dominate the product.

## 3. Optimize for real-world action

The application should reduce the gap between opening the product and doing the habit.

Avoid unnecessary screens, configuration, and content consumption before action.

## 4. Recovery is part of consistency

A missed day must not imply total failure.

The system should support:

- Honest missed check-ins
- Reflection without shame
- A clear next action
- Restarting quickly
- Measuring recovery, not only perfect streaks

## 5. Positive pressure, not humiliation

Accountability should feel supportive, clear, and serious.

The product must avoid:

- Public shaming
- Manipulative punishment
- Harassment
- Exposing sensitive failures
- Competitive mechanics that damage user well-being

## 6. Progress must be understandable

Users should quickly understand:

- What they committed to
- What is due
- What they completed
- What they missed
- What happens next
- How their partner or group is involved

## 7. Small loops before large systems

Validate the core accountability loop before building:

- Complex feeds
- Reward marketplaces
- Advanced matching
- Creator systems
- Large group mechanics
- Sophisticated reputation models

## 8. Mobile-first behavior

Habits happen away from a desk.

Core actions should be fast and comfortable on a phone:

- View today's commitment
- Check in
- Add brief evidence
- Respond to a partner
- Recover from a missed commitment

## 9. Trust and privacy by design

Users may share sensitive personal goals.

The product should provide clear control over:

- Who can see a habit
- Who can see evidence
- Who can comment
- Whether progress is public, group-only, partner-only, or private

## 10. Gamification supports meaning

Points, levels, streaks, badges, and rewards should reinforce consistent action and healthy recovery.

They should not replace intrinsic progress or encourage dishonest check-ins.

## 11. The first demo should be believable

The demo should show one complete accountability journey with polished state transitions.

Breadth is less important than coherence.
